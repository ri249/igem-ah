PROJECT DESIGN

The main objective of our iGEM project was to investigate if Vibrio natriegens could be a capable chassis for therapeutic protein production. But what exactly does capable mean?

THE IDEA

Speed, titer and yield are three competitive features for an industrial protein expression system. Research has shown that Vibrio natriegens can produce large amounts of protein quickly, thanks to its high rates of substrate uptake and growth.

However, the rate of protein production is only half the story. In large scale industrial applications efficiency is essential. One major hurdle in protein production is the downstream processing of the product. The proteins need to be extracted from the culture and purified. The number of downstream processing steps should be kept to the minimum to maximise production efficiency. So, we decided that a capable protein expression chassis should be capable of secreting the protein it produces to eliminate the need to lyse the cells before extracting the product. This minimizes cytoplasmic protein and DNA contamination.

VIBXPRESSO STRAIN

To meet the objective of competent solution, our aim was to create a modified Vibrio natriegens strain with an inducible Tat-pathway system in the spirit of previously described E. coli “TatExpress” strains (Browning et al., 2017). This allows rapid and agile production of different recombinant proteins in increased secretion titer.

We set out to optimize the usage of the Tat pathway in our protein production strain of V. natriegens. This included finding the best signal peptide for export and optimizing the concentration of the Tat-complex in relation to the amount of protein-to-be-exported produced. To find the best signal peptide for V. natriegens’ Tat pathway, we screened multiple known signal peptides for export activity by expressing gfp+signal fusion proteins from inducible plasmids. After expression we fractionated the cells and measured the gfp concentrations in cytoplasm and periplasm. We then compared the differences in concentration to identify the strongest secretion signal.

Unlike in E. coli, where the TatABC-proteins are expressed from a single operon, V. natriegens’ Tat-complex is encoded by two operons. One contains TatA and TatB, while the other contains only TatC. To induce the overexpression of the Tat-complex, we had to insert two additional inducible promoters into the genome of our V. natriegens strain, one upstream of both operons. We decided to go with sugar-inducible promoters because of their familiarity to most people in the field.

However, these sugar-inducible promoters have a major undesirable feature for our application: an all-or-nothing pattern of induction at low inducer concentrations. This phenomenon is caused by the upregulation of sugar transporters, induced by the uptake of said sugar. Thus, a positive feedback loop is created, leading to a heterogenous induction profile in the cell culture. This makes the expression level of genes under these promoters dose-dependent only on a whole-culture level, which is not optimal for our application of aiming to optimize the Tat-complex-to-product -ratio on a single-cell level.

To combat this phenomenon, a common approach is to decouple the sugar transporter from the positive feedback loop by moving it under a different promoter. However, due to time constraints, we came up with a different approach. V. natriegens is known not to metabolize lactose, and a quick BLAST -search did not identify any lactose transporters in its genome. Lactose analog IPTG can, however, enter the cell through other means. Thus, we hypothesized that V. natriegens has no positive feedback loop for the uptake of IPTG, meaning that promoters induced by it should be tunable on a single-cell level.

Data from E. coli shows that the relative expression levels of TatA:TatB:TatC are approximately 50:25:1. We didn’t have time to verify this in V. natriegens, but we assumed it to be reasonably correct. Thus, we wanted the induction level of tatC to be significantly lower than that of tatAB. We decided to insert the ptac-promoter upstream of tatAB and the weaker plac-promoter upstream of tatC. Both promoters are IPTG-inducible but ptac is roughly 10 times stronger than plac. These promoters are controlled by the lacI-repressor, which is also missing from the V. natriegens’ genome. To solve this, we decided to use the BioBrick___which contains the lacI-CDS followed by the ptac-promoter. Preceding the CDS, we included a constitutive promoter___and a ribosomal binding site___. As a result, we had inserted two IPTG-inducible promoters and their repressor into the genome.

We are aware that the system is far from perfect. Major problems include possible readthrough from the lacI to the TatAB as the operons are not separated by a transcriptional terminator. Another possible problem is the leakiness of the ptac and plac promoters. A common tactic to combat this is to upregulate the expression of lacI. We changed the start codon of the lacI-gene to ATG from GTG, which we hypothesized to be a repressive translational signal in V. natriegens based on the fact that tatB’s starting codon is GTG and its expression level is known to be lower than that of tatA in E. coli.

As a proof of concept for industrial applications, we produced human growth hormone in our system. The intronless hGH gene was fused with a 6xHis-tag and a Tat export signal sequence, and expressed from an inducible plasmid. The concentration of hGH was measured using his-tag affinity chromatography and Bradford assay.

Testing multiple levels of induction for both hGH and Tat, we created a model for protein expression and secretion in our system.

We also tried characterising two new promoters from V. natriegens’ native arabinose and rhamnose metabolism clusters. We cloned the promoters of operons homologous to E. coli’s araBAD and rhaBAD into a plasmid to test their induction. We also tried whether the E. coli promoters are compatible with V. natriegens’ regulatory proteins homologous to RhaS and AraC. The promoters were tested by constructing a PC201 derivative plasmid with a ygfp gene downstream from the promoter-to-be-tested.

Chromosomal edits were conducted using homologous recombination with linear vectors. This was supported by expressing V. natriegens native Tfox from an inducible plasmid during the editing phase. Tfox is a regulator of natural competence and thus its expression makes V. natriegens naturally competent. This increases the efficiency of homologous recombination, making the editing easier. The vectors were constructed with overlap extension PCR (OE-PCR).

We also deleted the deoxyribonuclease (dns) gene from our strain to increase transformation efficiency. The deletion has been shown not to affect the viability of V. natriegens in vitro.
